DOMAIN NAMING GUIDE

How to Find a Brandable Domain Name in 2026

Most good domain names are gone — but not all of them. The difference between a name that sounds professional and one that sounds like a placeholder usually comes down to a handful of rules. Here's what actually matters.

1. Shorter almost always wins

Names under 10 characters are easier to remember, easier to type on a phone, and less likely to get mistyped into someone else's site. If your keyword is long, look for a shorter synonym or a compound that reads naturally, rather than just shortening it into something that needs explaining.

2. Say it out loud before you buy it

A domain that looks fine written down can be a disaster spoken aloud — confusing when read over the phone, ambiguous in spelling, or accidentally awkward when two words run together. Read every candidate out loud before deciding.

3. The extension changes the perception, not just the price

  • .com — still the default for trust, especially outside tech circles
  • .io / .ai — signal "tech startup," well understood by developers and investors
  • .co — a reasonable .com alternative when the .com is taken
  • .app — fine for software products, less so for general businesses

None of these are universally "best" — it depends on your audience. A consumer app aimed at a general audience benefits more from .com than a developer tool does.

4. Avoid double letters, hyphens, and numbers

Each of these adds a small amount of friction every time someone tries to type or remember your domain. A hyphen especially tends to get dropped entirely when people say a domain out loud or write it down.

Quick test: if you'd have to spell the name out for someone over the phone, it's probably going to cost you traffic later.

5. Check availability before you fall in love with a name

It's common to spend an hour brainstorming a name, only to discover it's taken across every relevant extension. Checking availability early — and across several variations at once — saves that wasted effort.

6. A brandable name beats a "perfect" descriptive one

Exact-match keyword domains (like bestcoffeeshop.com) used to matter more for SEO than they do now. Search engines weigh content and relevance far more heavily today. A short, memorable, brandable name that people actually remember and share tends to outperform a long descriptive one in the long run.
